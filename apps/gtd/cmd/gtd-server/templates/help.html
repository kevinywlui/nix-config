How to use this app

Getting Things Done (GTD), David Allen's method, in a nutshell: your head is for having ideas, not holding them. Get every commitment out of your mind into one trusted system, decide what each one means, and keep lists you actually look at. The reward is "mind like water" — nothing nagging, because you trust the system to remember for you.

This app is that system, stored as plain todo.txt files. The whole method is five steps; each one is a screen here.

1 · Capture ＋ Capture

Whenever something has your attention — a task, an idea, a worry — dump it into your Inbox on the Capture screen. Don't organize, don't judge, don't even finish the thought. One item per capture, and move on. The only goal is to get it out of your head. Capture everything; you'll sort it out next.

2 · Clarify ⇄ Process

Later, empty the inbox on the Process screen. It hands you one item at a time and asks the only question that matters: what is it, and is it actionable?

  • Not actionable? Trash it, file it as Reference (worth keeping, no action), or park it in Someday / Maybe (not now, don't forget).
  • Actionable and under two minutes? Do it now and mark it done — chasing it through the system would cost more than just doing it.
  • A single next action? Name the very next physical step and the context it belongs to. Optionally defer it or give it a hard due date.
  • A project (more than one step)? Give it a tag and capture its very next action — a project isn't something you "do," it's an outcome you move one action at a time.
  • Waiting on someone else? Hand it off and track who owes you what.

Decide once, here, and the item leaves your inbox for good.

3 · Organize

Clarifying files each item into the right list automatically — you don't maintain these by hand:

  • Next actions, grouped by @context — the tools, places or people a task needs (@calls, @computer, @errands, @home). A "next action" isn't a flag you set; it's any actionable task parked on a context.
  • Projects — every multi-step outcome, flagged if it has no next action so it can't quietly stall.
  • Waiting — things others owe you.
  • Someday / Maybe and Reference live in their own files, out of the way.

4 · Reflect ↻ Review

The system only stays trustworthy if you look at it. Glance at your next actions through the day, and once a week run the Weekly review: it walks you down the whole loop on one read-only screen — empty the inbox to zero, scan your next actions, work the hard landscape (anything overdue, due today, or freshly woken from a t: defer), chase stale waiting items, land on every stalled project (one with no next action), and scan Someday / Maybe for anything ready to pull back in. The Files screen shows the exact todo.txt behind it all if you want to see precisely what's stored.

5 · Engage

Now just do the work with confidence. Open Next, pick the context you're in — at your computer, out running errands, on the phone — and choose from a short list of things you can actually do right now. No re-deciding, no overwhelm: the thinking already happened in Clarify.

Fixing things

  • Tap ✎ next to any item to edit its wording or tags.
  • Made a mistake? An ↶ Undo last change bar appears after any change and rolls back the last one.
  • Completed actions live on the Done screen; ↩ Restore brings one back to your active lists if you finished it by accident.

The shorthand, if you're curious

Everything is stored as todo.txt, so any todo.txt tool can read it. The GTD ideas map to a few inline tokens:

@inboxunprocessed capture
@calls @home …context lists
+ProjectNamea multi-step project
@waiting for:alicedelegated / awaiting someone
t:2026-07-01defer — dormant until that date
due:2026-07-01hard due date

You never have to type these — the Capture and Process screens add them for you — but you can if you like.
